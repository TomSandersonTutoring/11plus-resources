Odd ones out & matches

The friendliest way into non-verbal reasoning. You simply compare pictures: spot the shape that does not belong, or the one that fits best with a group.

What these are Worked example Most unlike Most like (3) Most like (2)

What these questions are

These puzzles show you a handful of small pictures and ask you to compare them. Sometimes four go together and one does not, and you find the odd one out. Sometimes a little group belongs together and you pick the extra picture that joins them. Either way, the skill is the same: notice what the pictures have in common, and what makes one different.

On this page you will meet three kinds:

  • Most unlike — five shapes, four share something. Find the one that does not belong.
  • Most like (3 figures) — three shapes go together. Pick the option that belongs with them.
  • Most like (2 figures) — the same idea, but with only two shapes to learn the rule from.

The six-point check

Almost every difference in non-verbal reasoning is one of six things. When a picture puzzle looks baffling, run through them in order and one will usually jump out:

  • Shape — how many sides; curved or straight?
  • Shading — white, shaded, or solid?
  • Size — large or small?
  • Number — how many shapes, dots or lines?
  • Position — where things sit, and which way up?
  • Rotation — has it been turned?

A worked example

Here is a “most unlike” question, taken slowly. Four of these five belong together; one does not.

Worked example · most unlike
Which shape is the odd one out?
  1. Do not grab the first difference you see. Go through the six-point check one at a time: shape, shading, size, number, then any marks inside.
  2. Shape. They are all different — circle, square, triangle, pentagon, hexagon. Because they are all different, shape cannot be the answer: an odd one out needs four the same and one different.
  3. Shading, size, number. All are shaded, all the same size, all a single shape. None of those singles anyone out.
  4. Inside. Four have a dot in the middle. One does not. That is the odd one out.
Answer: the hexagon — it is the only shape with no dot inside. The changing shapes were there to distract you.

Now your go

Three short sets, one for each kind. Tap a shape to answer and you will get an explanation straight away. Nothing is timed or saved, so get it wrong as often as you like.

A little tip: when the shapes all look different, that is usually the distraction. Keep running the six-point check until the real pattern shows itself.
